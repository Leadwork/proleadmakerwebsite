A bad cadence is worse than no cadence. It burns your domain, annoys your prospect, and makes future outreach harder. Most teams know this in theory and still over-fire in practice.

Here's how to design a cadence that earns meetings, not unsubscribes.

## The four cadence variables

Every cadence is a combination of four levers:

1. **Channels** (email, LinkedIn, phone, SMS, video).
2. **Touch frequency** (how often).
3. **Touch density** (how many channels per day).
4. **Sequence length** (how long the campaign runs).

Most teams over-tune frequency and ignore the other three.

## The reference cadence

Our default cadence for outbound is:

- **8 touches** across **3 channels** (email, LinkedIn, phone) over **18 days**.
- **Email-heavy** (4 of 8 touches), **LinkedIn-supporting** (3 touches), **phone-anchoring** (1 touch).
- **No two channels on the same day** unless the prospect is a tier-1 account.

That's it. Anything more is harassment; anything less leaves replies on the table.

## Why 18 days

Three reasons:

1. **Prospect inbox cycles.** 18 days hits two full work-week rhythms, catching prospects in different modes.
2. **Decision velocity.** Most B2B buying decisions move on 2–4 week cycles. 18 days is enough to be present without becoming background noise.
3. **Compound signals.** If a prospect doesn't reply by day 18, the signal-to-noise ratio of further touches plummets. Better to stop and re-add 90 days later with a new signal.

## What goes in each touch

The temptation is to use every touch to pitch. Resist.

A good 8-touch cadence has roughly:

- **3 pitch touches** (emails 1, 3, 5).
- **2 value touches** (email 2: resource; email 4: case study).
- **2 social/light touches** (LinkedIn engagement, voicemail).
- **1 breakup touch** (final email, 7–11% reply rate).

The value and social touches keep you in the inbox without exhausting goodwill.

## Cadence design mistakes

**Too many sends per inbox.** A single inbox sending 50 cold emails a day with 50 follow-ups will tip into Spam within two weeks. Spread across inboxes.

**No personalization beyond the first email.** Follow-ups must add new context: "Saw your team posted X yesterday" not "Just checking in."

**Identical timing window.** All emails leaving at 9:00am Tuesday gets pattern-detected by filters. Randomize within an hour.

**Auto-bumping without read confirmation.** If a prospect opened email 1 ten times, the follow-up should reference that activity, not pretend it didn't happen.

**Breakup email that whines.** "I guess we're not a fit" reads as desperate. The breakup should be matter-of-fact and offer a clean out.

## Channel-specific cadence notes

### Email
- Max 4 emails in 18 days from a single inbox.
- Never send Monday morning or Friday afternoon.
- Subject lines: 3–5 words, lowercase, no punctuation.

### LinkedIn
- 1 connection request, 1 InMail (if applicable), 1 DM follow-up.
- Engagement (comment on a post) is the most underrated LinkedIn touch.

### Phone
- 1 call attempt + voicemail, not three call-bombs in a day.
- Best windows: Tues/Wed/Thu, 10–11am or 3–4pm local.

### Other channels (video, SMS)
- Use sparingly, only for tier-1 accounts.
- Video DMs from a real person at the right moment can be the difference-maker for a $50k+ deal.

## How to measure a cadence

Track these per cadence variant:

- **Reply rate** by touch number.
- **Positive vs. negative replies.**
- **Meetings booked per 100 prospects.**
- **Unsubscribe rate** (should stay under 0.4%).
- **Spam complaint rate** (should stay under 0.1%).

If reply rate drops below 2%, the list or the messaging is the problem — not the cadence. Fix those before adding more touches.

## Signal-based cadence customization

The most effective optimization is matching cadence intensity to signal strength.

- **High-signal accounts** (just funded + hiring + champion identified) → tighter 12-day cadence, 4 channels, 10 touches.
- **Medium-signal accounts** → standard 18-day, 3-channel, 8-touch cadence.
- **Low-signal accounts** → 6-touch nurture only, monthly cadence.

That's how you get the most pipeline out of a fixed-size SDR team. Don't run a high-effort cadence on a low-signal prospect.
