Every cold-email failure traces back to one of three causes: bad list, bad copy, or bad infrastructure. The most common — and the most preventable — is bad infrastructure, and the heart of infrastructure is warmup.

This is the 30-day plan we run on every new client domain.

## What warmup actually does

Email warmup is the process of slowly building reputation on a new sending domain and inbox by sending small, varied, replied-to messages over weeks. The signals mailbox providers (Gmail, Outlook, Yahoo) use to decide whether you're a real sender are:

- Domain age and consistency.
- Email volume ramp (slow vs. spiky).
- Reply rate from recipients.
- Spam complaint rate.
- Bounce rate.

A new inbox sending 50 cold emails on day one fails on every one of those signals. A warmup tool simulates the right behavior until the inbox has a track record.

## The 30-day plan

### Days 1–3: Domain setup

- Buy the domain. Use a respected registrar.
- Set up MX records pointing to your email provider (Google Workspace, Outlook 365, or specialty provider like Maildoso).
- Configure **SPF**, **DKIM**, and **DMARC** records. Verify with `mxtoolbox.com`.
- Create 2–3 inboxes per domain.
- Do not send anything yet.

### Days 4–14: Warmup ramp

- Plug each inbox into a warmup tool (Smartlead, Instantly, Mailwarm, Warmupinbox).
- Start at 5–10 simulated emails per day per inbox.
- Increase by 5 per inbox every 2 days until you hit 25–30 daily warmup messages.
- Watch reply rate. It should hold at 35–50% (warmup tools simulate replies).
- Spot-check the inbox manually every few days — make sure messages are landing in Primary, not Promotions.

### Days 15–21: Light real-send

- Start real cold sending at 5 emails per inbox per day, max.
- Continue warmup at 20+ per day per inbox in parallel.
- Use highly verified, hand-picked test prospects for the first sends — you want some real replies.
- Watch deliverability metrics daily.

### Days 22–30: Scale-up

- Increase real-send by 2–3 emails per inbox per week.
- By day 30, healthy inboxes should be doing 25–35 cold emails per day.
- Keep warmup running at 15–20 per day per inbox indefinitely.

## What kills a warmup

The four most common warmup-killers:

**Spiking sends.** Going from 5 to 50 cold emails per day on the same inbox. Mailbox providers detect the spike and throttle.

**Skipping warmup entirely.** "I'll just start sending and see how it goes." You'll see how it goes: straight to Spam.

**Using recycled domains.** A cheap "aged" domain often comes with reputation baggage. Buy new and warm it yourself.

**Warmup tools but no real engagement.** Warmup tools generate fake replies. They're a starting boost, not a complete solution. You also need a few real replies in the first 30 days.

## Inbox provider differences

**Google Workspace** is the strictest warmup. Plan for 30 days minimum, no shortcuts. Gmail's filters are sophisticated.

**Microsoft Outlook 365** is even stricter on cold senders in 2026. Some teams have switched to Google Workspace specifically because Outlook deliverability has gotten so hard.

**Specialty providers** (Maildoso, Mailforge, Inboxbear) are often easier to warm because they offer pre-warmed inboxes — at a premium cost. Worth it if you're scaling fast.

## How many inboxes you need

The math:

- One inbox can sustainably send 30–50 cold emails per day, post-warmup.
- A 100-email-per-day cold campaign needs 2–4 inboxes.
- A 500-per-day campaign needs 10–20 inboxes across 3–5 domains.

Most teams under-provision and then wonder why their sending suddenly stops working at scale.

## Ongoing warmup

Warmup never fully stops. Even after 30 days, keep 15–20 warmup messages per day per inbox flowing in the background. They keep reply rates high enough to maintain reputation.

Stop warmup entirely and your inbox reputation slowly decays over 60–90 days. Restart it before the next big campaign.

## The shortcut that doesn't exist

There is no legitimate 7-day warmup. There is no provider that can warm 50 inboxes in a week. Any service promising that is selling you a domain-destroying timeline.

The boring, mechanical 30-day process is the only one that consistently works. Build it in.
