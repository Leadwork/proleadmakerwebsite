There are two kinds of B2B prospect lists. The first costs $30 and gets you 5,000 rows of "John Smith / Manager / john@company.com" that lands you in Spam and burns your domain. The second takes real work, has 600 rows, and books 40+ meetings.

This is the workflow for the second kind.

## Step 1: Lock the ICP before you list-build

Most teams skip this. Don't.

Before pulling a single contact, write down five things:

1. The **company profile** you're targeting (industry, size, geography, revenue band).
2. The **2–3 signals** that indicate buying intent (hiring, funding, tech stack).
3. The **buyer titles** with budget authority.
4. The **champion titles** who feel the pain.
5. The **disqualifiers** (anything that makes a company a bad fit).

If you can't write all five in 10 minutes, your ICP isn't tight enough yet. Tighten it before list-building, not after.

## Step 2: Source companies first, contacts second

The mistake most people make is searching for contacts directly. The right workflow is to find the *companies* that match your ICP, then find the right *contacts* inside those companies.

Tools we use to source companies:

- **LinkedIn Sales Navigator** — filter by headcount growth, industry, location, recent activity.
- **Apollo / ZoomInfo / Clay** — firmographics + tech stack + funding data.
- **Crunchbase** — funding rounds, founding dates, M&A activity.
- **BuiltWith / Wappalyzer** — companies using specific tools.
- **Job boards** (Indeed, LinkedIn Jobs) — hiring signals.

Target output: a deduped list of 1,000–5,000 companies that pass your ICP filter.

## Step 3: Stack the signals

Take the company list and layer signals on top:

- Date of last funding round.
- Number of relevant job postings in last 30 days.
- Tech stack matches (or absence of competitor tool).
- Recent executive hires in relevant roles.

Score each company on signal count. Anything with 2+ signals goes to the top of the queue.

## Step 4: Identify contacts inside each company

Now and only now, find people. For each company, you usually want:

- 1 **champion** (the person who feels the pain).
- 1 **decision-maker** (the person who signs).
- 0–1 **gatekeeper** (an ally or someone who can route you).

Don't grab "all VPs at this company." That dilutes the list and confuses your outbound.

## Step 5: Verify everything

This is where 80% of bought lists fall apart. Every contact gets:

- **Email verification** (Reoon, Million Verifier, NeverBounce).
- **Title verification** against current LinkedIn.
- **Domain check** — is the company website live? Is it the actual company or a shell?

Anything that fails verification gets dropped. We typically trim 15–25% of the raw list at this stage.

## Step 6: Enrich for personalization

The hottest lists carry one extra column: a "personalization hook" — a fact you noticed that's worth referencing in the opening line.

Examples:

- "Posted on LinkedIn about scaling outbound 4 days ago."
- "Hired 3 SDRs this month."
- "Mentioned competitor X in a podcast."

This single column moves reply rates from 2% to 6%.

## Step 7: Format for outreach

A well-formatted list has, at minimum:

- First name, last name.
- Title (current, verified).
- Company name, domain, employee count.
- Verified business email.
- LinkedIn URL.
- Personalization hook.
- Source signal.

Deliver in Excel, Google Sheets, or CSV. Skip the proprietary formats.

## Common list-building mistakes

**Over-sourcing.** A 50,000-row list isn't 10x better than a 5,000-row list. It's just 10x more inbox burn and 10x more cleanup work later.

**No deduplication across campaigns.** Hitting the same prospect with three different reps' sequences within a quarter trashes your domain and your brand.

**Skipping verification.** A 10% bounce rate on a 5,000-email send equals 500 hard bounces — enough to trash inbox placement for a month.

**No re-verification cadence.** A list that was 98% valid 6 months ago is now ~75% valid. Re-verify quarterly.

## The build-vs-buy decision

If you have a single SDR + one campaign per quarter, building lists in-house is fine.

If you have 5+ reps or run 3+ campaigns per quarter, the SDR salary spent on Apollo searches and spreadsheet cleanup is gone. Buy a built list and put the SDR time back on calls and replies.
