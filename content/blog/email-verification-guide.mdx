Email verification is the most boring step in cold outreach and the one that most determines whether your campaign succeeds or burns your domain.

If you send to an unverified list, here's what happens: a measurable percentage of those emails are dead. Each one becomes a hard bounce. Bounces above 2% drop inbox placement. Above 5%, you get throttled. Above 10%, your domain reputation collapses inside a week.

A 10,000-email send on a 70%-deliverable list isn't just an underperforming campaign — it's a domain-destroying event.

## The four kinds of bad emails

Verification is about catching these before they hit your queue:

**Invalid / non-existent.** The mailbox simply doesn't exist. SMTP returns 550. Hard bounce, immediate damage.

**Catch-all.** Domain accepts email for any local part. Looks valid to most verifiers but might still bounce or go unread. Treat as risky.

**Role-based.** `info@`, `sales@`, `admin@`, etc. Usually monitored by no one, often go to a black hole inbox. Skip unless you have specific intent.

**Disposable / temporary.** `mailinator.com`, `tempmail.io`. Used by people who don't want real follow-up. Skip.

## How email verification works under the hood

A real verification check runs four sequential steps:

1. **Syntax check.** Does the address match RFC 5321 format?
2. **DNS / MX check.** Does the domain have MX records pointing somewhere real?
3. **SMTP handshake.** Open a connection to the mailbox provider, issue `HELO`, `MAIL FROM`, `RCPT TO`. Check the response code.
4. **Catch-all probe.** Send `RCPT TO` for a random local part on the same domain. If accepted, the domain is a catch-all.

The reliability of step 3 depends on the provider. Gmail responds honestly. Microsoft (Outlook, Office365) has been aggressively anti-verification since 2023 — they accept and silently bounce. That's why a single-vendor verification is no longer enough.

## Why one verifier isn't enough in 2026

Microsoft's 2023+ stance broke most single-vendor verifiers. The fix:

- **Triple-check** through Reoon, Million Verifier, and NeverBounce.
- An email that passes all three is "safe."
- An email that passes two of three is "risky" — send if you must, with caution.
- An email that fails two or more is "drop."

For our agency clients, we triple-verify everything before delivery. The extra cost per email is a fraction of a cent. The cost of skipping it is a destroyed domain.

## What to do with catch-all emails

Catch-alls are the trickiest tier. Some are real (whole company shares `support@` style routing), some are dead.

Our rule: ship catch-alls only when the contact has been signal-verified another way — recent LinkedIn activity, recent press mention, recent job board posting from the company. If we can confirm the person exists and is currently in role, a catch-all email has a much higher chance of being live.

We label catch-alls separately in the deliverable so the outbound team can send them in their own segment.

## Re-verification cadence

A list's quality decays over time:

- **0–30 days:** ~98% deliverable.
- **30–90 days:** ~92% deliverable.
- **90–180 days:** ~80% deliverable.
- **180+ days:** ~65% deliverable, much worse if industry has high churn.

If you're using a list older than 60 days, re-verify before sending. Always.

## Practical workflow

For every list we deliver:

1. Initial verification pass through three providers.
2. Catch-all probe + signal cross-check.
3. Role-account flagging (separate tab).
4. Re-verification on any contact older than 30 days.
5. Output: clean list + risky list + dropped list (so the client sees the math).

That's the standard. Anyone selling lists without that workflow is selling you a domain-destroying event in a spreadsheet.
