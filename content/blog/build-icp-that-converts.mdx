Every B2B team has an ICP document. Most of them are useless.

The typical ICP is three bullet points — "SaaS companies, 50–500 employees, in North America" — pinned to a Notion page nobody opens. It produces lists. It does not produce pipeline.

A real ICP is operational. It tells you which 5,000 companies to focus on, in what order, with what message, and why.

## The four layers of a usable ICP

### Layer 1: Fit (firmographic)

This is the easy part. Industry, size, geography, revenue band. It's the filter that says "this company could plausibly buy from us."

Most teams stop here. That's the mistake.

### Layer 2: Need (problem signal)

What observable event indicates this company needs your solution right now?

For a sales enablement tool, that might be: just hired three new SDRs. For a logistics platform, it might be: opened a second warehouse. For an HR tool: scaled headcount by 20% in the last 6 months.

Need signals are public if you know where to look — job boards, press releases, LinkedIn updates, SEC filings, hiring patterns.

### Layer 3: Urgency (trigger event)

Need without urgency is a "let's revisit in Q3" reply. Urgency comes from a fresh trigger:

- A new executive in a relevant role (the average new exec replaces 30% of their stack in their first 90 days).
- A funding round.
- A failed competitor implementation (visible in Glassdoor reviews and LinkedIn).
- A regulatory or compliance deadline.

### Layer 4: Power (decision authority)

Even the most qualified company won't convert if you're talking to the wrong person. For your ICP to be operational, you need to know who at the company has both:

- The pain (champion).
- The budget (economic buyer).

For most B2B sales, those are two different people, and you should be working both in parallel — not sequentially.

## The "100 best-fit accounts" exercise

Once a quarter, do this:

1. Pull your closed-won deals from the last 12 months.
2. Strip the 20% smallest deals (they probably aren't your ICP — they're just whoever signed).
3. For the rest, list everything they have in common at the *moment they bought*: hiring patterns, tech stack changes, exec turnover, fundraising.
4. Score every active prospect against those traits.
5. The top 100 by score is your account list for the quarter.

This single exercise replaces three months of "let's brainstorm our ICP" meetings.

## Common ICP mistakes

**Too broad.** "SaaS companies" includes 200,000 organizations. You can't go after all of them. Narrow until your list is 500–5,000 companies, max.

**Demographic-only.** "CMOs at $20M ARR SaaS companies" is a label, not a profile. Without need + urgency layers, you're emailing 80,000 people who happen to share a job title.

**Built from wishes, not data.** Sales teams want to target the Fortune 500. The data usually says you actually close mid-market companies in the $5M–$50M range. Build from where you actually win.

**Frozen.** Your ICP from 18 months ago is wrong. Re-run the closed-won exercise every quarter.

## ICP → message fit

Once your ICP is layered, your outbound message writes itself. The opener should reference the signal that triggered the outreach:

> "Saw you opened a Boston office last month and posted three Operations roles in the last 30 days — we usually see logistics teams hit this stage and start hitting our edge case around warehouse pick-pack-ship handoffs. Curious if that's where you're heading."

That's a 1-line email that books meetings because it proves you noticed something specific. The signal is doing the qualification work.

## The output

When your ICP is truly operational, your sales team should be able to answer these three questions in under 10 seconds:

1. "Should we go after this account?"
2. "Why now?"
3. "Who do we talk to first?"

If the answer to any of those is fuzzy, the ICP isn't done.
