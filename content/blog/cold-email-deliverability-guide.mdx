Deliverability is the only metric that matters in cold email. If your email lands in Spam or Promotions, every other optimization — subject lines, copy, timing — is wasted.

This guide walks through the 2026 stack: what's changed, what's required, and what most teams still get wrong.

## What changed in 2024–2026

Three rule changes from Google, Yahoo, and Microsoft tightened the screws on bulk senders:

- **DMARC is now required** for senders moving more than 5,000 messages a day to Gmail or Yahoo. A `p=none` policy is the minimum.
- **One-click unsubscribe** must be honored within 2 days for any commercial messaging.
- **Spam complaint rates above 0.3%** trigger gradual delivery throttling. Above 0.5%, your domain reputation collapses inside a week.

Cold email is not bulk marketing, but providers don't always distinguish. If you can't keep complaint rates low and authentication clean, you lose access to the inbox.

## The four-layer deliverability stack

### 1. Domain and infrastructure

- **Use a secondary domain.** Never send cold from your primary corporate domain. Buy `getproleadmaker.com` or `try-proleadmaker.com` and send from there.
- **Set up SPF, DKIM, and DMARC** on the sending domain. Verify with `mxtoolbox.com/SuperTool.aspx`.
- **One inbox per 30–50 emails/day**, max. If you need to send 500 emails a day, you need 10–17 inboxes spread across multiple domains.

### 2. Warmup

A brand-new inbox should not send a single cold email for the first 14 days. Use a warmup service (Smartlead, Instantly, Mailwarm) that runs peer-to-peer interactions to build reputation gradually:

- Days 1–7: 5–10 warmup messages a day.
- Days 8–14: 20–30 a day.
- Day 15+: 5–10 real cold emails per day, increasing by 5 per week up to 30–50.

Never skip warmup. Cold-starting a domain is the fastest way to land permanently in Promotions.

### 3. Content signals

Modern spam filters score content on dozens of dimensions. The cheap wins:

- **No images, no tracking pixels, no link shorteners** in the first email.
- **No more than one link**, and never `bit.ly`-style links.
- **No spam triggers** like "free," "limited time," "urgent," "act now."
- **Reasonable plain-text formatting.** Avoid all-caps subject lines.
- **Conversational length** — 60 to 110 words is the sweet spot for replies and for filters.

### 4. List hygiene

This is where most teams fail. A list with even 5% catch-all or invalid emails will tank deliverability inside two weeks because bounces compound.

- Verify every email before sending. We use Million Verifier, Reoon, and NeverBounce on top of our own validation.
- Remove catch-all addresses unless you have specific intent on the contact.
- Re-verify lists older than 60 days.

## Monitoring deliverability

Spot trouble before it spirals:

- **Inbox placement tests** (GlockApps, Mailreach) weekly.
- **Google Postmaster Tools** for domain reputation, spam rate, and IP reputation.
- **Reply rate by inbox** — a sudden drop on one inbox signals throttling.

## Common mistakes that kill deliverability

1. **Sending from your main domain.** One spam complaint can damage your transactional email.
2. **Buying old, recycled domains.** Always check Wayback Machine and domain age.
3. **Skipping DMARC.** Even `p=none` is enough to satisfy bulk-sender rules.
4. **No-reply addresses.** Mailbox providers treat them as a signal of low-quality bulk mail.
5. **Sending too many emails too fast.** A new inbox doing 50/day on day one is a red flag.

## The 30-day deliverability runbook

If you're starting fresh:

1. Buy 3–5 sending domains (week 1).
2. Set up SPF, DKIM, DMARC on every domain (week 1).
3. Create 2–3 inboxes per domain (week 1).
4. Start warmup on every inbox (week 1–3).
5. Verify and segment your first campaign list (week 3).
6. Send 5 emails/day per inbox for 5 days (week 4).
7. Scale 5–10/day per inbox per week thereafter.

Deliverability isn't sexy. It's the boring foundation everything else sits on.
