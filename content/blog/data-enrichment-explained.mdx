Most B2B teams have a "list problem" that's actually an enrichment problem. They have rows of names and companies — they just don't have the data they need to do anything useful with them.

Enrichment fixes that. Done right, it turns a stale CRM into an outbound goldmine.

## What enrichment is (and isn't)

Enrichment is the process of taking a known data point — usually a name + company, or an email — and finding everything else you need to act on it.

It is *not* list-building from scratch. It assumes you already have a starting record.

The classic enrichment fields:

- **Person:** Full name, current title, department, seniority, LinkedIn URL, business email, mobile number.
- **Company:** Domain, employee count, industry, location, revenue band, funding history.
- **Signals:** Recent job changes, recent funding, hiring activity, tech stack.

## When you need enrichment

Three common situations:

**Webform leads.** Someone fills out your "request demo" form with `john@startup.io`. You need their title, company size, and seniority before the AE picks up the phone.

**Stale CRM cleanup.** Records from 18+ months ago are 30–40% wrong. Re-enrich before you sequence them again or they'll bounce.

**Acquired lists.** You bought a list (or got one from an event), and it's 5 fields wide when you need 15.

In each case, the existing record is the seed. Enrichment grows it.

## The enrichment stack

For each missing field, there's a different best-in-class provider. The teams that get the cleanest enrichment use waterfall logic — try the cheapest provider first, fall back to the next-best if it doesn't return a hit.

A typical waterfall:

1. **Apollo / Clay** for firmographics + basic person data.
2. **ZoomInfo** for missing direct dials and senior-exec data.
3. **Hunter / Snov** for email lookups.
4. **Reoon / Million Verifier** to validate emails post-enrichment.
5. **Manual LinkedIn pass** for the 10% of records that automated tools miss.

The manual pass is where most agencies skip work and most quality is lost.

## What we add (beyond the basics)

For our clients, we layer two enrichment dimensions that vendors typically don't:

**Signal context.** For each contact: are they posting actively on LinkedIn? Did they just change jobs? Is their company hiring in a relevant role? These are the personalization hooks that drive reply rates.

**Buying-cohort mapping.** For each company, who else on the team is part of the buying cohort? A CMO doesn't make a decision alone — there's a Director of RevOps and a VP of Sales in the same conversation.

A good enriched record gives you the whole buying-cohort surface, not just one contact.

## Common enrichment mistakes

**Single-vendor.** Apollo alone misses ~25% of seniors. ZoomInfo alone misses startup data. Always waterfall.

**No re-enrichment cadence.** A contact's title and email are 95% accurate on day one. By month 12, they're 65% accurate. Re-enrich quarterly.

**Over-enriching the wrong records.** Don't burn enrichment credits on tier-3 accounts. Score first, enrich the top 30%.

**Skipping verification post-enrichment.** Enriched emails still need verification — about 8–12% of even good-vendor emails are dead within a quarter.

## The output

A well-enriched record has the data fields the outbound rep needs *and* a personalization hook the rep can use without research.

Example:

```
Name: Sarah K.
Title: VP of RevOps (current — verified Jan 2026)
Company: Acme SaaS, 240 employees, Series B 2025
Email: sarah.k@acmesaas.com (verified, 98% confidence)
LinkedIn: linkedin.com/in/sarahk
Phone: +1 415-XXX-XXXX
Signal: posted about Salesforce → HubSpot migration on Mar 18
Hook: "Saw your post on the HubSpot migration — same situation we ran with..."
```

That's a record an SDR can act on in 30 seconds. The opposite — "Sarah K. / VP / sarah.k@acmesaas.com" — is a record that takes 10 minutes of manual research before any outreach.

Enrichment is just systematizing that 10 minutes across thousands of records.
