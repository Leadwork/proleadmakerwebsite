Running outbound for one client is hard. Running outbound for 5+ clients in parallel — different ICPs, different domains, different cadences, different reporting cadences — is a different sport entirely.

This is what the top B2B agencies actually run under the hood.

## The infrastructure stack

For each client, an agency typically maintains:

- **2–5 sending domains** dedicated to the client.
- **6–15 inboxes** spread across those domains.
- **One sending tool** (Smartlead, Instantly, Heyreach, Lemlist) per client.
- **Warmup running 24/7** on every inbox.
- **Verification credits** on Reoon, Million Verifier, NeverBounce.

That's $1,200–$3,500/month of tooling per client. Agencies that try to share infrastructure across clients eventually burn one client's reputation for another's. Don't.

## The list pipeline

The hardest operational problem at scale is list management. Three flows:

1. **Net-new lists** from list-building vendors (or built internally).
2. **CRM re-engagement** from the client's existing data.
3. **Re-verification** of older lists before reuse.

Each list goes through:

- ICP filter pass.
- Triple-verify pass.
- Dedupe against all prior client sends in last 12 months.
- Tag with source, signal, cohort.
- Load into the sending tool.

Skip dedup and you'll hit the same prospect with three reps' sequences over a quarter. That's the fastest way to lose a client account.

## The cadence library

A top agency runs maybe 8–12 cadence templates across all clients:

- New-customer launch.
- Mid-cycle follow-up.
- Re-engagement (90+ days dormant).
- Trigger-based (post-funding, post-hire).
- Event-driven (conference attendees, webinar signups).

Each template has been A/B tested across thousands of sends. Agencies that build new cadences for every client from scratch burn 30% of their margin on prep work.

## Reporting that retains clients

The agencies that retain clients longest send weekly reports that include:

- Meetings booked (the only metric the client cares about).
- Reply rate by sequence (so the client sees you're optimizing).
- Domain reputation status (so the client knows you're protecting their data).
- Next-week plan (what's changing and why).

A 4-bullet weekly email beats a 20-tab dashboard nobody opens.

## The team

A 5-client agency typically needs:

- **1 strategist** (manages the relationship, owns the strategy).
- **2 SDRs / list researchers** (build and maintain lists).
- **1 deliverability / ops manager** (manages domains, infrastructure, warmup).
- **1 copywriter** (writes and tests cadences across all clients).

The mistake most agencies make is hiring more SDRs and skipping the deliverability ops manager. The single person who keeps inboxes healthy is the highest-leverage hire at scale.

## Where list-building services fit

Few agencies build all their lists in-house at scale — the labor cost is too high.

The economic model that works:

- **Strategy and persona work** → in-house.
- **Net-new list-building, enrichment, verification** → outsourced to a specialist (us or similar).
- **Cadence execution and reporting** → in-house.
- **Meeting handoff to client** → in-house.

This split lets the agency keep the high-margin strategy work and outsource the labor-intensive research.

## The growth ceiling

The agency growth ceiling isn't usually demand — it's operational. Most agencies cap out at 5–8 simultaneous clients because the ops complexity above that point breaks systems.

The agencies that scale past 10 clients have:

- A list partner who delivers triple-verified, signal-based lists on a 5-day SLA.
- An ops manager managing deliverability full-time.
- Templated reporting that takes 15 minutes per client per week.
- A real onboarding playbook (week 1–4 detailed runbook).

Everything else is a variation on those four pillars.
