Most B2B teams still buy lead lists the same way they did in 2014: pick an industry, pick a company size, pick a job title, hit export. The result is a spreadsheet of cold strangers and a response rate hovering between 0.5% and 1%.

Signal-based prospecting flips this on its head. Instead of asking *who could buy*, it asks *who is showing they need to buy right now*.

## What counts as a buying signal

A buying signal is any observable change in a company or person that correlates with purchase intent. The strongest ones cluster around four categories.

**Hiring signals.** A SaaS company posting three Account Executive roles is scaling outbound. A logistics firm hiring a VP of People probably needs HR tooling. Job boards are the most underused intent dataset in B2B.

**Funding and revenue signals.** A Series A often means the company just bought a new CRM, a new analytics stack, or a new outreach platform within 90 days. Series B usually means they're replacing what didn't scale from Series A.

**Technographic signals.** Companies that just installed HubSpot are 4x more likely to buy a sales enablement tool than the average prospect. Companies that removed a tool are looking for a replacement.

**Trigger events.** A new CMO, a new office, a product launch, an acquisition. Each one rewires the decision-making cohort and creates a window where new vendors get a fair hearing.

## Why generic lists fail

A "list of US SaaS CEOs at 50–200 employee companies" is functionally an audience of 80,000 people who happen to share a label. They have no shared context with you, no reason to expect your email, and 90% of them are mid-contract with a competitor.

You can send those 80,000 emails. You will hit spam folders, burn your domain reputation, and book a handful of meetings — but the cost per booked meeting will be 5–10x what it should be.

## What a signal-based list looks like

A real signal-based list is much smaller and much hotter. For one of our SaaS clients, we delivered a 600-contact list with three layered signals:

1. Company raised seed or Series A in the last 12 months.
2. Currently hiring an SDR or Head of Sales.
3. Job titles match a "buyer + champion + economic owner" triad.

That list booked 40+ calls in the first week of outreach. The win wasn't volume — it was that every contact had a reason to take the meeting.

## How to start without a research team

You don't need a 10-person research bench. You need a process:

- Pick the **two** signals most predictive of buying *your* solution.
- Use LinkedIn Sales Navigator and a tool like Apollo or Clay to filter.
- Manually verify the top 200 — emails, titles, role context.
- Track replies by signal type and double down on whichever signal converts.

A generic list at 0.8% reply rate costs about $180 per booked meeting in outreach labor.

A well-built signal-based list at 4.5% reply rate costs about $42 per booked meeting — even though the per-lead research cost is 3x higher.
